# Campaign Frameworks Reference

Detailed email sequence structures, timing, and example copy patterns for each campaign type.

---

## Back in Stock Campaign

### Psychology
The subscriber already expressed intent — they wanted this item and couldn't get it. That prior desire is your biggest asset. Don't re-sell the product; remind them why they wanted it and create urgency around limited availability.

### Sequence Structure

**Email 1 — The Alert** (Trigger: Item restocks)
Send immediately. Speed matters — the first email should land within minutes of restock.

```
Subject: {Product name} is back (finally)
Preview: We only got {number} this time — your size is in stock.

Hey {Name},

Remember {product name}? It's back in stock.

Fair warning: we got {quantity} units this time, and {number} people were 
waiting for this restock. First come, first served.

[CTA: Grab mine before it's gone]

— {Brand}

P.S. Last time, this sold out in {timeframe}. Just saying.
```

**Annotations:**
- Subject uses product name for instant recognition + "(finally)" validates their wait
- Preview adds scarcity with a specific number
- Body is short because the decision is already half-made
- CTA uses "mine" (ownership language) + urgency
- P.S. adds social proof through past behavior

**Email 2 — The Nudge** (12-24 hours later, only if not purchased)

```
Subject: {X}% of restock already claimed
Preview: {Product} is going fast — here's what people are saying about it.

Hey {Name},

Quick update: since we restocked {product} yesterday, {percentage}% has 
already sold.

Here's why people love it:

⭐ "{Short testimonial}" — {Customer name}
⭐ {Star rating} from {number} reviews

[CTA: Get yours while it lasts]

If it sells out before you grab one, I can add you back to the waitlist — 
but I'd rather you didn't have to wait again.

— {Brand}
```

**Annotations:**
- Subject uses percentage (specific social proof)
- Body leads with stock update, then adds testimonials
- Offer to re-add to waitlist shows respect for their autonomy (not pushy)

**Email 3 — Last Chance** (48-72 hours later, only if not purchased)

```
Subject: Last call: {product} is almost gone again
Preview: {Number} left. After that, we're not sure when it's coming back.

Hey {Name},

Not trying to pressure you, but I want to be upfront: we're down to 
{number} units of {product}, and I can't guarantee when we'll restock next.

If you're on the fence:
- {Address top objection — e.g., "Free returns within 30 days if it's not right"}
- {Address second objection — e.g., "Ships tomorrow, arrives by {date}"}

[CTA: Grab the last few]

If it's not for you right now, no worries. Want me to notify you if we 
get more? [Secondary CTA: Yes, notify me next time]

— {Brand}
```

**Annotations:**
- Opens with honesty ("not trying to pressure you") which ironically increases trust
- Addresses top 1-2 objections directly
- Offers a soft exit (notify me) — respects the reader, reduces unsubscribes

## Abandoned Cart Campaign

### Psychology
They were interested enough to add to cart. Something stopped them — price concern, distraction, comparison shopping, or friction. Each email addresses a different barrier.

### Sequence Structure

**Email 1 — The Gentle Reminder** (1-4 hours after abandonment)

```
Subject: You left something behind
Preview: Your cart is saved — pick up where you left off.

Hey {Name},

Looks like you didn't finish checking out. No worries — your cart is 
saved with {product(s)} ready to go.

[Show cart contents — image + name + price]

[CTA: Complete my order]

Need help? Reply to this email and a real human will get back to you.

— {Brand}
```

**Annotations:**
- No discount yet — many carts are abandoned by distraction, not price
- Shows the actual items (visual reminder triggers desire)
- "Reply to this email" signals real customer service

**Email 2 — The Objection Handler** (24 hours later)

```
Subject: Still thinking about {product}?
Preview: Here's what {number} other customers said about it.

Hey {Name},

Still deciding on {product}? Totally fair — here's what might help:

⭐ "{Brief testimonial}" — {Customer name}
📦 Free shipping on orders over ${threshold}
↩️ {Return policy — e.g., "30-day hassle-free returns"}

{Number} people bought this in the last week.

[CTA: Add to cart and checkout]

— {Brand}
```

**Annotations:**
- Acknowledges the hesitation ("totally fair")
- Stacks proof + logistics + risk reversal
- Social proof through recent purchase data

**Email 3 — The Incentive** (48-72 hours later)

```
Subject: {X}% off your cart — today only
Preview: We saved your {product} and added a little something extra.

Hey {Name},

Your cart's been sitting here for a couple days, so here's a nudge: 
use code {CODE} for {X}% off {product}.

This code expires at midnight tonight.

[CTA: Use my {X}% off code]

After that, your cart goes back to full price and I can't guarantee 
{product} will still be in stock.

— {Brand}

P.S. Code {CODE} — copy it now so you don't forget.
```

**Annotations:**
- Discount comes only in Email 3 (don't train customers to abandon for discounts)
- Real deadline on the code
- P.S. repeats the code (highest-visibility spot)

## Welcome / Onboarding Sequence

### Psychology
First impressions set expectations. The welcome sequence builds trust, demonstrates value, and guides the new subscriber toward a first meaningful action. Don't sell immediately — earn the right to sell.

### Sequence Structure

**Email 1 — Welcome + Quick Win** (immediately after signup)

```
Subject: Welcome to {Brand} — here's your {promised incentive}
Preview: Plus one quick thing that'll make your first {experience} better.

Hey {Name},

Welcome! Here's your {discount code / free resource / access link}:

{DELIVER THE THING THEY SIGNED UP FOR}

One quick tip to get started: {single actionable step that delivers 
immediate value}.

That's it for now. Over the next week, I'll send you {number} more 
emails with {what they'll get — tips, guides, exclusive access}. 
They're worth reading.

Talk soon,
{Sender name}
{Brand}
```

**Annotations:**
- Deliver promised incentive immediately (trust-building)
- Set expectations for sequence length and value
- One quick win, not a feature tour

**Email 2 — Core Value Demo** (Day 2)

Focus: Show the single most valuable thing about your product/service through a use case, story, or quick tutorial.

**Email 3 — Social Proof + Deeper Feature** (Day 4)

Focus: Customer story or testimonial + introduce a feature that solves a specific problem.

**Email 4 — Objection Handling** (Day 7)

Focus: Address the #1 reason people don't buy/activate. FAQ format works well.

**Email 5 — Conversion Push** (Day 10-14)

Focus: Clear CTA to purchase/upgrade/activate. Recap value, add urgency if there's a trial expiring or welcome offer ending.

## Product Launch Campaign

### Psychology
Build anticipation before launch. Make early supporters feel like insiders. Create a sense of event around the release.

### Sequence Structure

**Email 1 — Teaser** (7-10 days before launch)
- Subject angle: "Something new is coming"
- Job: Create curiosity, hint at the category without full reveal
- CTA: "Get notified first" or "Join the waitlist"

**Email 2 — Reveal / Preview** (1-3 days before launch)
- Subject angle: "First look at {product}"
- Job: Full reveal, key benefits, early-access or launch-day offer
- CTA: "Be first in line" or "Set a reminder"

**Email 3 — Launch Day** (day of launch)
- Subject angle: "{Product} is live"
- Job: Product is available, launch offer details, urgency
- CTA: "Shop now" or "Get {product}" with direct product link

**Email 4 — Follow-up** (2-3 days post-launch)
- Subject angle: "How {product} is being received" or stock/availability update
- Job: Social proof from early buyers, address any remaining hesitation
- CTA: Same destination, urgency-focused copy

## Post-Purchase Campaign

### Psychology
The moment after purchase is the highest-trust moment in the customer relationship. Use it to reduce buyer's remorse, encourage product adoption, and plant seeds for the next purchase.

### Sequence Structure

**Email 1 — Confirmation + Delight** (immediately after purchase)
- Beyond the transactional confirmation — add a personal touch
- Set expectations for delivery/access
- Suggest one thing they can do right now

**Email 2 — Onboarding / Usage Tips** (3-5 days after delivery/access)
- Help them get maximum value from the purchase
- Quick-start guide or top 3 tips
- Link to support if needed

**Email 3 — Review Request** (7-14 days after delivery)
- Ask for feedback (genuine, not just for marketing)
- Make it easy (star rating, one-click)
- Thank them regardless

**Email 4 — Cross-sell / Replenishment** (30+ days after purchase)
- Recommend complementary products based on their purchase
- Or remind about replenishment if consumable product
- Loyalty program mention if applicable

## Re-engagement / Winback Campaign

### Psychology
They were once interested. Something changed — your emails lost relevance, they found an alternative, or life got busy. Approach with humility, not guilt.

### Sequence Structure

**Email 1 — The Check-in** (after 30-60 days of inactivity)

```
Subject: Is this still the right inbox?
Preview: We haven't heard from you in a while — just checking in.

Hey {Name},

We noticed you haven't opened our last few emails. No hard feelings — 
inboxes get crowded.

But just in case you're still interested, here's what's new:
- {1 compelling update}
- {1 piece of value — guide, tip, new feature}

[CTA: Yes, keep me in the loop]

If you'd rather not hear from us, that's OK too:
[Secondary: Unsubscribe]

— {Brand}
```

**Email 2 — Value Reminder** (3-5 days later, only if opened Email 1)

Show what they're missing — not features, but outcomes. Customer stories work well here.

**Email 3 — Final Email** (7-10 days later)

```
Subject: Should I stop emailing you?
Preview: Last email unless you say otherwise.

Hey {Name},

This is the last email I'll send unless you want to stay on the list.

No guilt trip — I just don't want to clutter your inbox if it's not 
useful anymore.

[CTA: Keep sending me emails]

If I don't hear from you, I'll remove you from the list in 7 days. 
You can always re-subscribe at {website} if you change your mind.

— {Brand}
```

**Annotations:**
- Honest, respectful tone increases trust even if they leave
- Clear deadline creates natural urgency without being manipulative
- Easy path to stay AND easy path to leave

## Promotional / Sale Campaign

### Sequence Structure

**Pre-Sale Teaser** (1-3 days before)
Build anticipation. Early access for loyal customers.

**Sale Launch** (Day 1)
Clear offer, clear deadline, clear CTA. Hero product or category.

**Mid-Sale Reminder** (Day 2-3)
Highlight bestsellers, what's selling fast, customer picks.

**Last Chance** (Final day)
Countdown, real scarcity, final push.

**Extended** (Day after, optional)
Only if genuine — "We extended by 24 hours due to demand." Use sparingly or lose credibility.

## Newsletter / Regular Send

### Structure
Lead with value, not promotion.

**Pattern:**
1. One main story / insight / tip (70% of the email)
2. One secondary link or recommendation (20%)
3. One soft CTA or product mention (10%)

**Consistency rules:**
- Same sender name every time
- Same send day/time (build habit)
- Same general format (readers learn where to look)
- Subject lines that signal what's inside, not clickbait

## Cross-Campaign Rules

### Priority & Suppression
When a subscriber qualifies for multiple flows simultaneously:

1. **Transactional emails** always send (order confirmations, shipping)
2. **Triggered behavioral emails** (abandoned cart, back in stock) take priority over scheduled sends
3. **Suppress promotional emails** during active behavioral sequences
4. **Never stack more than 1 email per day** from your brand (excluding transactional)
5. **Post-purchase buyers exit** abandoned cart and back-in-stock flows immediately

### Frequency Caps
- New subscribers: max 3 emails in first week (excluding transactional)
- Active subscribers: 2-4 emails per week depending on engagement
- At-risk subscribers: reduce to 1 per week before re-engagement
- After re-engagement attempt: if no response to 3 emails, stop

## Deliverability & Compliance

### Deliverability Basics
- Authenticate your domain (SPF, DKIM, DMARC)
- Clean your list quarterly (remove hard bounces, long-term unengaged)
- Warm up new sending domains/IPs gradually
- Monitor spam complaint rate (keep under 0.1%)
- Use a recognizable sender name consistently

### Compliance Checklist
- Physical mailing address in footer (CAN-SPAM)
- Working unsubscribe link in every email (CAN-SPAM + GDPR)
- Honor unsubscribe within 10 days (CAN-SPAM requires this)
- Consent records for GDPR subscribers
- Don't use misleading subject lines (CAN-SPAM)
- Identify the message as an ad if it is one (CAN-SPAM)
